Exp 1 & 2 — Merge Sort / Quick Sort
Divide & Conquer O(n log n)
  • Q: 3 steps of D&C? A: Divide, Conquer (recurse), Combine.
  • Q: Merge Sort recurrence? A: T(n)=2T(n/2)+O(n) → O(n log n).
  • Q: Merge Sort best/worst/avg? A: All O(n log n) — always splits evenly.
  • Q: Quick Sort best/worst/avg? A: Best/Avg O(n log n), Worst O(n²).
  • Q: When does Quick Sort hit worst case? A: Sorted/reverse array + last-element pivot → unbalanced split.
  • Q: Avoid worst case? A: Random pivot or median-of-three.
  • Q: Stability? A: Merge=stable, Quick=not stable.
  • Q: Space complexity? A: Merge O(n) extra, Quick O(log n) stack.
  • Q: In-place? A: Merge=No, Quick=Yes.
  • Q: Why Quick faster in practice? A: Cache locality, in-place, low constant factor.
  • Q: Lomuto vs Hoare partition? A: Lomuto=pivot last elem single pass(your code); Hoare=two pointers, fewer swaps.
  • Q: Best sort for linked lists? A: Merge Sort (no random access needed).
  • Q: Lower bound comparison sorts? A: Ω(n log n).
  • Q: Graph shape time vs n? A: Slight upward curve (n log n), not linear/exponential.
Exp 3 — Fractional Knapsack (Greedy)
GreedyO(n log n)
  • Q: Greedy approach? A: Locally optimal choice each step, hoping for global optimum.
  • Q: Greedy choice property & optimal substructure? A: Local best leads to global best; optimal built from optimal subproblems.
  • Q: Why greedy works for fractional, not 0/1? A: Fractional allows partial items; 0/1 can't split, ratio-greedy may block better combo.
  • Q: Time complexity? A: O(n log n) — dominated by sorting by ratio.
  • Q: Skip sorting by ratio? A: Greedy fails, no optimal guarantee.
  • Q: Proof of correctness? A: Exchange argument — swap low-ratio for high-ratio never decreases profit.
  • Q: Fractional vs 0/1? A: Fractional=can split item; 0/1=take fully or not at all.
  • Q: Can DP solve fractional? A: Yes but unneeded — greedy already optimal & faster.
Exp 4 — Prim's Algorithm (MST)
GreedyO(V²) matrix / O(E log V) heap
  • Q: MST definition? A: Min-weight subset of edges connecting all vertices, no cycle, V-1 edges.
  • Q: Greedy choice in Prim's? A: Pick min-weight edge from tree-set vertex to outside vertex.
  • Q: Prim vs Kruskal? A: Prim grows one tree from a vertex; Kruskal picks globally smallest edge + Union-Find to avoid cycles.
  • Q: When is Prim's better? A: Dense graphs (matrix rep efficient).
  • Q: Disconnected graph? A: Gives only partial spanning tree of that component.
  • Q: Negative weights ok? A: Yes, MST doesn't care about sign.
  • Q: MST always unique? A: No, if equal-weight edges exist.
  • Q: Cut property? A: Min edge crossing any cut belongs to some MST.
  • Q: Real life use? A: Power line/network design, min total cost.
Exp 5 — Topological Sort (DFS)
DFSO(V+E)
  • Q: Definition? A: Linear order of vertices: for edge u→v, u comes before v.
  • Q: Precondition? A: Graph must be a DAG (no cycle).
  • Q: Time complexity? A: O(V+E) list, O(V²) matrix (your code).
  • Q: Why push to stack after visiting neighbors? A: Node added only after all dependents processed → correct finish order.
  • Q: Unique order? A: No, multiple valid orders can exist.
  • Q: Kahn's algorithm? A: BFS-based: pick indegree-0 vertices repeatedly, decrease neighbors' indegree.
  • Q: Detect cycle? A: If sort can't include all vertices, cycle exists.
  • Q: Applications? A: Task scheduling, course prerequisites, build systems.
Exp 6 — Dijkstra's Algorithm
GreedyO(V²) / O(E log V)
  • Q: Problem solved? A: Single-source shortest path, non-negative weights.
  • Q: Time complexity? A: O(V²) matrix, O(E log V) with min-heap.
  • Q: Why fails with negative weights? A: Finalized vertex never revisited, but later negative edge could shorten path.
  • Q: Algorithm for negative weights? A: Bellman-Ford, O(VE).
  • Q: Greedy choice? A: Pick unvisited vertex with min known distance.
  • Q: Dijkstra vs BFS? A: BFS only unweighted graphs; Dijkstra handles weights.
  • Q: Dijkstra vs Floyd-Warshall? A: Dijkstra=single source, Floyd=all pairs.
  • Q: Detect negative cycle? A: No, Dijkstra can't — gives wrong result.
  • Q: Role of visited array? A: Marks finalized vertices, ensures greedy correctness.
Exp 7a/7b — Floyd-Warshall & Warshall's Transitive Closure
DPO(V³)
  • Q: Floyd-Warshall solves? A: All-pairs shortest path.
  • Q: Complexity? A: O(V³) time, O(V²) space.
  • Q: Algorithm type? A: Dynamic Programming.
  • Q: Recurrence? A: d[i][j]=min(d[i][j], d[i][k]+d[k][j]).
  • Q: Detect negative cycle? A: Yes — if d[i][i] < 0 after running.
  • Q: What does k represent? A: Intermediate vertex tried in path i→j.
  • Q: Warshall vs Floyd? A: Warshall=boolean reachability; Floyd=numeric min distance.
  • Q: Transitive closure use? A: Check reachability between all vertex pairs.
Exp 8 — 0/1 Knapsack (DP)
DPO(n·W) Pseudo-Poly
  • Q: Why DP not greedy? A: Items can't split; ratio-greedy doesn't guarantee global optimum.
  • Q: Recurrence? A: sol[i][j]=max(sol[i-1][j], sol[i-1][j-wt[i]]+val[i]) if wt[i]≤j.
  • Q: Complexity? A: O(n×W) time/space — pseudo-polynomial (depends on W's value).
  • Q: Pseudo-poly meaning? A: Depends on magnitude of input values, not just count n.
  • Q: Backtrack selected items? A: Compare sol[i][j] vs sol[i-1][j]; if diff, item i picked, j-=wt[i].
  • Q: Space optimize to O(W)? A: Use 1D array, update j in reverse order.
  • Q: Base case? A: sol[i][j]=0 when i=0 or j=0.
  • Q: Top-down vs bottom-up? A: Top-down=recursion+memo; Bottom-up=iterative tabulation.
Exp 9 — PCB Drilling (TSP-type Greedy Heuristic)
NP-HardO(n!) brute / O(n²2ⁿ) DP
  • Q: Problem type? A: Like Travelling Salesman Problem — visit all holes minimizing travel.
  • Q: TSP brute force complexity? A: O(n!).
  • Q: Held-Karp DP complexity? A: O(n²·2ⁿ).
  • Q: Is nearest-neighbor optimal? A: No — greedy heuristic only, not guaranteed optimal.
  • Q: Approximation algorithm? A: Near-optimal solution in poly time for NP-Hard problems.
  • Q: Why TSP is NP-Hard? A: No poly-time algo known; reduces from Hamiltonian Cycle.
  • Q: Branch & bound vs brute force? A: B&B prunes bad paths early using bounds, faster in practice.
  • Q: Real life apps? A: Drilling path, delivery routing, circuit design.
Exp 10 — N-Queens (Backtracking)
BacktrackingO(N!)
  • Q: Backtracking def? A: Build incrementally, abandon path as soon as invalid, explore others.
  • Q: Backtrack vs Brute vs Greedy? A: Brute=tries everything; Greedy=picks best, never reconsiders; Backtrack=tries+undoes on failure.
  • Q: Time complexity? A: O(N!) worst case.
  • Q: place() checks? A: Same column (x[j]==i) AND same diagonal (|x[j]-i|==|j-k|).
  • Q: Why that diagonal formula? A: Equal column-diff and row-diff means same diagonal.
  • Q: State space tree? A: Tree of all placements tried; branches pruned when invalid.
  • Q: N=2 or N=3? A: No solution exists for either.
  • Q: Other techniques? A: Branch & bound, bitmask optimization.
  • Q: Backtracking vs DP? A: Backtrack explores valid paths only, no subproblem reuse; DP reuses via memo (not useful here).
General DAA Theory — Always Asked
QuestionAnswer
Algorithm? Good traits?Finite well-defined steps to solve a problem. Traits: correctness, finiteness, definiteness, efficiency, well-defined input/output.
Big O, Omega, Theta?O = upper bound (worst case). Ω = lower bound (best case). Θ = tight bound (exact growth, both bounds).
Time vs Space complexity?Time = runtime growth with input size. Space = memory growth with input size.
Best/Worst/Average case?Best = min steps for ideal input. Worst = max steps for hardest input. Average = expected steps over all inputs.
Solving recurrences?Substitution method, Recursion tree method, Master theorem.
Master theorem?T(n)=aT(n/b)+f(n): compare f(n) to n^(log_b a). Smaller→Θ(n^log_b a). Equal→Θ(n^log_b a · log n). Larger→Θ(f(n)).
Greedy vs D&C vs DP vs Backtracking?Greedy: local optimal, no backtrack. D&C: split, solve independent, combine. DP: optimal substructure + overlapping subproblems, stores results. Backtracking: explore all, undo on failure.
Optimal substructure?Optimal solution of problem contains optimal solutions of subproblems.
Overlapping subproblems?Same subproblems solved repeatedly — DP avoids recompute via memo/table.
Memoization vs Tabulation?Memoization = top-down recursion + cache. Tabulation = bottom-up iterative table fill.
P, NP, NP-Hard, NP-Complete?P = solvable in poly time. NP = verifiable in poly time. NP-Hard = at least as hard as hardest NP problems (e.g. TSP). NP-Complete = in NP AND NP-Hard.
Approximation algorithm?Near-optimal solution in poly time for NP-Hard problems where exact is too costly.
Backtracking vs Branch & Bound?Backtracking prunes on validity only. Branch & Bound also uses a cost bound to prune branches that can't beat current best — used for optimization.
Adjacency Matrix vs List?Matrix: O(V²) space, O(1) edge check, good for dense graphs. List: O(V+E) space, better for sparse graphs.
Spanning Tree / MST?Spanning tree = connects all vertices, no cycle. MST = spanning tree with min total weight.
BFS vs DFS?BFS: queue, level-by-level, O(V+E), shortest path in unweighted graphs. DFS: stack/recursion, depth-first, O(V+E), cycle detection, topo sort.
Bottleneck capacity?Minimum edge capacity along a path — it limits the maximum flow that can pass through that path. Used in Max-Flow (Ford-Fulkerson): bottleneck = min residual capacity on the augmenting path.
Max-flow min-cut theorem?Maximum flow from source to sink = capacity of the minimum cut separating them.
Directed vs Undirected?Directed = one-way edges. Undirected = bidirectional. Topo sort/Dijkstra use directed; Prim's MST uses undirected.
DAG?Directed Acyclic Graph — directed, no cycles. Required for topological sort.
Stable vs Unstable sort?Stable preserves relative order of equal elements. Unstable doesn't guarantee this.
In-place vs not in-place?In-place = O(1) extra space. Not in-place = extra space proportional to input (e.g. Merge Sort O(n)).
Why Ω(n log n) lower bound?Decision tree model: n! orderings need ≥ log₂(n!) ≈ n log n comparisons to distinguish.
Pseudo-poly vs poly?Poly = depends on count of inputs (n). Pseudo-poly = depends on magnitude of input values (like W in knapsack).
